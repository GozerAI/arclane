  ## Competitive Landscape
  
  ### 1. Common Sense Media
  **Offer:** Free reviews of apps, games, movies, and TV shows plus screen time guides. Revenue from donations, licensing, and brand partnerships.
  **Pricing:** Free (with donation prompts). No paid membership.
  **Messaging strength:** Institutional authority. AAP-cited, school-district trusted, enormous SEO footprint.
  **Messaging weakness:** Cold and encyclopedic. Reads like a nonprofit filing, not a parent talking to a parent. Zero community or accountability layer. Advice is broad and generic â€” "2 hours per day" with no implementation help.
  **Differentiation opportunity:** They tell parents *what* the rules should be. They never help parents actually enforce them, build buy-in with kids, or troubleshoot the fight that happens at 7pm on a Tuesday.
  
  ---
  
  ### 2. Screenagers (Delaney Ruston / Tilt Learning)
  **Offer:** Documentary films, a school screening program, and a weekly newsletter called "Tech Talk Tuesdays." Sells educator/school kits.
  **Pricing:** Films ~$15-25 for home streaming. School programs vary. Newsletter free.
  **Messaging strength:** Strong on teen risk narrative â€” anxiety, social media harm, dopamine. Well-produced. School credibility.
  **Messaging weakness:** Almost entirely focused on **teens**, not 5-12 year olds. The framing is fear-based and reactive. No app recommendations, no structured challenges, no community for parents doing the work together.
  **Differentiation opportunity:** There is a clear gap at the younger age bracket. Parents of 5-12 year olds are trying to build healthy habits *before* the teen crisis hits. This business owns that window.
  
  ---
  
  ### 3. 1000 Hours Outside (Ginny Yurich)
  **Offer:** Book, podcast, social media community, and a tracking app centered on getting kids 1,000 hours outdoors per year as a counterweight to screens.
  **Pricing:** Book ~$18. App free with optional paid tier (~$4.99/month). Large free community on Facebook/Instagram.
  **Messaging strength:** Extremely shareable, positive, and aspirational. The "1000 hours" goal is concrete and sticky. Massive organic reach.
  **Messaging weakness:** Philosophically anti-screen but offers no practical screen management tools. Parents who can't go all-in on outdoor time feel excluded. No evidence base cited â€” it's inspirational, not instructional.
  **Differentiation opportunity:** Parents who follow 1000 Hours Outside still have screens in their house. They need a practical toolkit, not just inspiration. This business can be the "what to do with the hours that *aren't* outside."
  
  ---
  
  ### 4. Aha! Parenting (Dr. Laura Markham)
  **Offer:** Evidence-based parenting blog and book series covering emotional regulation, discipline, and connection. Some screen time content buried in the archive.
  **Pricing:** Free blog. Books ~$16-18 on Amazon. No paid membership.
  **Messaging strength:** Licensed psychologist author. Warm tone. Strong on emotional science and parent-child connection.
  **Messaging weakness:** Screen time is not their focus â€” it's one topic among hundreds. No structured program, no app recommendations, no community accountability. Content is generic across ages 0-18.
  **Differentiation opportunity:** Dr. Markham has the credibility but not the focus. A blog that applies the same evidence-based lens *exclusively* to screen time for the 5-12 bracket will outrank her on that specific intent.
  
  ---

  **Offer:** Free pledge campaign encouraging parents to delay smartphone ownership until 8th grade. Newsletter and talking-point resources.
  **Pricing:** Free. Donor supported.
